## The person who already built something {#the-person-who-already-built-something}

Every mid-market organization we walk into has the same thing waiting for us. Somewhere in the building — usually in operations, sometimes in finance, occasionally in HR — there is a person who figured out Claude six months ago and has been quietly using it to do work that used to take hours.

She built a contract review assistant. He automated his weekly reporting. They connected Claude to a shared drive and created something that answers questions about internal policy faster than searching the wiki.

Nobody asked them to do it. Nobody knows the full extent of what they built. And the work is genuinely good.

This person is the starting point. Not the governance framework. Not the vendor evaluation. Not the AI strategy deck.

## Why process-first fails {#why-process-first-fails}

The conventional approach to AI adoption starts with strategy. Hire a consultant. Map the organization. Identify use cases. Build a governance framework. Select a vendor. Run a pilot. Evaluate. Scale.

That process takes six to twelve months before anyone builds anything. And the use cases identified in a boardroom rarely match the ones that produce value on the ground.

The people doing the actual work know where the pain is. They know which report takes four hours every Monday. They know which approval workflow has seventeen steps and should have three. They know because they live inside it.

> "The use cases identified in a boardroom rarely match the ones that produce value on the ground."

When you start with process, you get a strategy document. When you start with people, you get a working application — and the strategy reveals itself.

## The interview that changes everything {#the-interview-that-changes-everything}

In the first week of every engagement, we sit down with the people who do the work. Not the executives. Not the department heads. The person who processes the invoices. The coordinator who builds the schedules. The analyst who runs the numbers.

We ask three questions. What do you do every day that feels repetitive? What have you already tried to automate? What would you build if you could describe it in plain English and have it work?

The answers are always specific, always practical, and always better than what a strategy consultant would have proposed. These are the workflows worth automating first — not because they score highest on a priority matrix, but because the person who owns them is already motivated to make them work.

## From one person to the whole team {#from-one-person-to-the-whole-team}

The first builder becomes the proof point. Her automated contract review saves four hours a week. That is not a pilot result on a slide. That is a colleague showing her team what she built over lunch.

The second builder sees the first and thinks: I could do that for my process. The third sees both and starts asking how.

Our job is to make that replication safe, governed, and fast. The Claude environment we deploy has guardrails. The SDLC plugin enforces review. The MCP connections are configured against real systems with real permissions.

By day 30, the infrastructure exists for anyone on the team to build what the first person built — without the risk of ungoverned AI usage spreading across the organization.

Start with the person who already built something. Learn what she knows. Then give everyone else the same capability, inside a system you control.
